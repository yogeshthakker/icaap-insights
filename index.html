I have sat across from SAMA examiners more than once. And I have reviewed ICAAP documents from institutions that clearly understood the structure of the process — but not the substance.

The result is always the same: a document that looks complete, but fails to answer the question the regulator is actually asking: "Do you truly understand your own risk — or have you documented what you think we want to see?"

The SAMA Supervisory Analysis Blueprint
01
The ICAAP and the business plan speak different languages
Capital planning disconnected from strategic planning
High severity
▼
The business plan projects SME book growth of 40% over three years. The ICAAP models a 15% growth scenario. They were written by different teams, in different quarters, anchored to different macro assumptions. SAMA examiners identify this within the first thirty minutes of a supervisory visit.

If your capital projections are not stress-tested against the same assumptions as your strategy, the ICAAP ceases to be a forward-looking management tool — it becomes a compliance artefact. That is not what Pillar 2 was designed to produce, and SAMA's supervisory expectations have evolved accordingly.
◆
Remediation
→Run a mandatory joint scenario-setting workshop between Risk and Finance at the start of every ICAAP cycle — align macro, growth, and margin assumptions before either team begins modelling
→The ICAAP base scenario must be numerically identical to the base case in the strategic plan — same revenue, same credit growth, same cost trajectory
→Stress scenarios should directly test the downside of your specific strategic bets — real estate concentration, SME expansion, digital lending — not generic macroeconomic shocks
→Include a one-page reconciliation table in the ICAAP document showing exactly where assumptions align and where they deliberately diverge, with rationale
◆  What examiners actually ask
"Walk me through how your growth assumptions in the business plan are reflected in your capital projections. What happens to your capital adequacy ratio if growth comes in 20 percentage points above your base case?"
02
Pillar 2 add-ons are regulatory minimums dressed up as internal assessments
Capital quantification lacks institution-specific justification
High severity
▼
Walk into most KSA banks' ICAAP documents and the Pillar 2 add-ons are reverse-engineered from the regulatory floor. The capital number was anchored first; the stress narrative was written to justify it second. Experienced examiners have reviewed hundreds of these documents. They know the pattern immediately.

A genuine Pillar 2 assessment quantifies risks that Pillar 1 does not fully capture — concentration risk, IRRBB, operational risk beyond the standardised charge, reputational and strategic risk. The add-on must be a number your own scenarios produced, not a number anchored to a minimum.
◆
Remediation
→Run bespoke stress scenarios for each material Pillar 2 risk — document the methodology and assumptions, not just the capital output
→For concentration risk: calculate your HHI (Herfindahl-Hirschman Index) for single-name, sector, and geographic exposures — translate the excess concentration to a capital equivalent under your worst stress scenario
→For IRRBB: model the impact of a 200bps parallel shift and a steepening scenario on both NII and EVE — SAMA expects quantification, not narrative description
→For operational risk: supplement the standardised charge with a scenario analysis for your top 3–5 operational risk events specific to your business model and geography
→Your add-on rationale must stand alone: "We hold X% of Tier 1 for concentration risk because our top-10 exposures represent Y% of portfolio, and under our adverse scenario, stressed losses total Z"
◆  What examiners actually ask
"Your IRRBB add-on on page 14 — which rate scenario generated that number? What was your EVE sensitivity? And how does your concentration add-on reflect the real estate sector weight in your book?"
03
The Board owns the signature, not the process
Governance is documented but not embedded
Med–high
▼
SAMA's ICAAP guidelines are explicit: the Board must own the process, not merely approve the output. In practice, most institutions present a polished 80-page document to the Board Risk Committee three days before the sign-off meeting. The Board approves. The governance box is ticked.

SAMA examiners have begun asking Board members direct questions during supervisory reviews. If a Board member cannot articulate the key stress scenarios, the material risks, or why the capital buffer is set at its current level — that is an immediate finding, regardless of how well the document reads on paper.
◆
Remediation
→Involve the Board Risk Committee at the scenario design stage — they should challenge your stress assumptions before models are run, not after numbers are finalised
→Prepare a Board executive summary that is genuinely readable in 20 minutes — key risks, key scenarios, capital headroom under each scenario, and management action triggers
→Run a 30-minute ICAAP briefing for Board members ahead of each approval cycle — not a training session, a structured briefing on the 5 questions SAMA is likely to ask them
→Document Board challenge specifically in committee minutes — questions raised, management responses given, and any resulting changes to assumptions. This is the evidence trail SAMA looks for
◆  What examiners actually ask
"If we were to speak separately with your Board Risk Committee Chair, could they walk us through your three stress scenarios and explain the capital implications of your adverse case?"
The underlying pattern

All three weaknesses share the same root cause: the ICAAP is treated as a regulatory submission rather than a genuine management tool.

The institutions that receive the cleanest SAMA supervisory reviews are the ones where the CRO can walk into the examination discussion without the document — because the numbers and narratives are alive in how the bank is actually run, not archived in a PDF that surfaces once a year.

That is the standard worth building towards. And it begins with the quality of the conversation between Risk, Finance, and the Board — long before the document is written.
